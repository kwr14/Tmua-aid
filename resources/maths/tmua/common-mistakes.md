# TMUA Common Mistakes

This note is about preventable mistakes.

Most TMUA losses do not come from not knowing any mathematics at all. They come from using the wrong method, missing structure, or making a small logical slip under time pressure.

## 1. Expanding Too Early

What goes wrong:

- you expand a structured expression and turn a short problem into a long one
- you lose the factor form that actually reveals the answer

Better habit:

- factor first
- look for a substitution
- ask whether the expression is really quadratic in disguise

Typical warning signs:

- repeated brackets
- repeated powers such as `2^x`
- expressions like `x + 1/x`

## 2. Ignoring The Graph

What goes wrong:

- you treat a root-count question as pure algebra
- you miss that the question is really asking about intersections or turning points

Better habit:

- ask what the graph looks like
- translate equations into intersections
- use turning points to limit the number of possible roots

Classic trap:

- finding one algebraic solution path and forgetting to ask how many solutions are even possible

## 3. Misusing The Discriminant

What goes wrong:

- using the discriminant in a situation that is not truly quadratic
- forgetting what `b^2 - 4ac = 0` means geometrically

Better habit:

- use it only after the equation is clearly in quadratic form
- connect the algebra to the picture:
- positive means two intersections
- zero means tangency or repeated root
- negative means no real intersection

## 4. Forgetting Domain Restrictions

What goes wrong:

- solving an equation correctly but keeping impossible values
- ignoring restrictions from square roots, logarithms, denominators, or trig definitions

Better habit:

- note restrictions before solving
- check every final candidate against the original expression

Fast examples:

- denominator cannot be zero
- inside a square root must be non-negative
- inside a logarithm must be positive

## 5. Losing Solutions Or Creating Fake Ones

What goes wrong:

- squaring both sides and creating extra roots
- dividing by an expression that might be zero
- canceling factors without checking the special case

Better habit:

- state the case you are excluding
- substitute answers back into the original equation

Useful rule:

- any step that is not reversible demands a final check

## 6. Bad Interval Counting In Trigonometry

What goes wrong:

- finding the general form of solutions but counting the wrong number in the stated interval
- assuming symmetry gives extra solutions without checking

Better habit:

- mark the exact interval
- sketch or picture the unit-circle positions
- list actual values in range if the interval is short

Classic trap:

- solving for `3x` correctly and then forgetting to convert back to `x`

## 7. Using The Wrong Sequence Formula

What goes wrong:

- treating a geometric sequence as arithmetic
- using the infinite geometric sum formula when `|r| >= 1`

Better habit:

- identify the structure first
- check ratio or difference explicitly
- test convergence before touching the infinite-sum formula

Most important filter:

- infinite geometric sum only works when `|r| < 1`

## 8. Missing Similar Triangles In Geometry

What goes wrong:

- trying lengthy coordinate or trigonometric methods when the diagram is simpler
- overlooking repeated angles or parallel-line angle facts

Better habit:

- sketch clearly
- mark equal angles
- ask whether two triangles have the same shape before calculating anything

Classic clue:

- tangents, parallel lines, or shared acute angles in right triangles

## 9. Doing Exact Computation When Comparison Is Enough

What goes wrong:

- spending too long calculating an exact value when the question only asks which option is largest or which statement is true

Better habit:

- compare sign, size, monotonicity, or rough magnitude first
- use elimination aggressively

TMUA reward:

- the fastest correct route often comes from reasoning, not complete computation

## 10. Confusing Necessary And Sufficient Conditions

What goes wrong:

- proving one direction and assuming the reverse
- treating “if” as “if and only if”

Better habit:

- translate statements carefully
- test the reverse separately
- look for counterexamples

Good question:

- does this condition guarantee the result, or merely make it possible?

## 11. Reading Too Fast

What goes wrong:

- missing words like `distinct`, `integer`, `positive`, `exactly`, `at least`, or `for all`
- solving a nearby question instead of the actual one

Better habit:

- underline the instruction mentally before solving
- reread the final sentence before choosing an option

These small words often control the entire method.

## 12. Not Checking Units Of Thought

What goes wrong:

- mixing algebraic and geometric assumptions carelessly
- treating a diagram as exact when it is only schematic

Better habit:

- use the diagram for relationships, not measurement
- justify equal lengths and equal angles from mathematics, not appearance

## 13. Failing To Use Multiple Choice Properly

What goes wrong:

- ignoring that the options can help
- doing the question as though it were a blank-page proof

Better habit:

- check whether rough reasoning eliminates several options
- plug in a candidate if direct derivation is too slow
- use the form of the options to infer what the exam expects

## 14. Careless Algebra Under Pressure

What goes wrong:

- sign slips
- miscopied terms
- wrong expansion
- arithmetic errors after correct setup

Better habit:

- write fewer lines, not more
- prefer structured methods
- if the algebra becomes long, ask whether the method is wrong

Warning sign:

- if a TMUA multiple-choice question is turning into a page of algebra, there is often a shorter route

## 15. Not Learning The Right Lesson From A Wrong Answer

What goes wrong:

- marking a question wrong and moving on
- recording only the final correct answer instead of the missed idea

Better habit:

- write the trigger you should have seen
- classify the mistake:
- concept
- recognition
- logic
- algebra
- careless reading

This is what improves future performance.

## A Short Pre-Paper Warning List

Before each practice paper, remind yourself:

- do not expand blindly
- sketch when the shape matters
- count solutions carefully
- check domains
- use the discriminant only in a true quadratic
- check `|r| < 1` for infinite sums
- reread the final sentence
- exploit the options

## Final Thought

Strong TMUA performance is often about avoiding predictable losses.

If you can remove these repeated mistakes, your score improves even before you learn any new mathematics.
