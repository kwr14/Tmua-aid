# TMUA Topic Drill Plan

This plan turns the TMUA library into a practical revision routine.

Use it if you want structured preparation rather than randomly opening papers.

## How To Use This Plan

- Work in timed blocks.
- After each block, review method, not just correctness.
- Keep a mistake log.
- Reattempt missed questions after a gap, not immediately.

Suggested rhythm:

- `25 to 40 minutes` for a topic drill
- `15 to 25 minutes` for review
- `1 full paper block` each week once you are comfortable

## Phase 1: Rebuild Core Skills

Goal:

- restore fluency in the main techniques before heavy timed practice

Order:

1. quadratics and algebra structure
2. functions and graphs
3. trigonometry
4. geometry and circles
5. sequences and series
6. calculus basics
7. number structure and logic

### Block 1: Quadratics And Algebra Structure

Focus on:

- factorisation
- completing the square
- discriminant
- substitution
- inequalities and sign analysis

What to practise:

- questions where expressions hide a quadratic structure
- line-parabola intersections
- parameter questions changing the number of roots

What success looks like:

- you spot “quadratic in disguise” quickly
- you know when not to expand

### Block 2: Functions And Graphs

Focus on:

- transformations
- turning points
- monotonicity
- graph intersections
- range and domain thinking

What to practise:

- sketching rough shapes from equations
- counting solutions by graph behavior
- matching algebraic conditions to pictures

What success looks like:

- you use graph language naturally
- you can explain why an equation has `0`, `1`, `2`, or more solutions

### Block 3: Trigonometry

Focus on:

- core identities
- exact values
- counting trig solutions in intervals
- rewriting everything in one consistent form

What to practise:

- solving trig equations by factorising
- interval-based solution counting
- geometric trig in triangles

What success looks like:

- you stop guessing symmetry
- you count only the solutions that actually lie in the interval

### Block 4: Geometry And Circles

Focus on:

- similar triangles
- tangent-radius facts
- Pythagoras
- area methods
- circle equations

What to practise:

- drawing a clean sketch first
- deciding between algebraic and geometric methods
- choosing the shortest area route

What success looks like:

- you see the hidden right triangle or similar-triangle structure early

### Block 5: Sequences And Series

Focus on:

- arithmetic sequences
- geometric sequences
- finite sums
- infinite sums
- recursive patterns

What to practise:

- spotting constant ratio or difference
- checking convergence first
- writing the first few terms before generalizing

What success looks like:

- you identify sequence type almost immediately
- you do not use infinite-sum formulas when they do not apply

### Block 6: Calculus Basics

Focus on:

- derivatives and stationary points
- definite integrals
- area interpretation
- simple optimization

What to practise:

- deciding when geometry beats symbolic integration
- reading graph meaning from derivative information
- connecting stationary points to root counts

What success looks like:

- you treat integrals as areas when possible
- you use turning points to reason about graphs

### Block 7: Number Structure And Logic

Focus on:

- divisibility
- parity
- prime factors
- logical statements
- counterexamples

What to practise:

- rewriting conditions into prime-factor form
- testing universal claims with small cases
- separating necessary and sufficient conditions

What success looks like:

- you attack these with structure, not random testing

## Phase 2: Mixed Topic Drills

Goal:

- get better at identifying the method quickly

Format:

1. Choose `8 to 12` mixed questions from different papers.
2. Give yourself a short time limit.
3. Record for each question:
- topic
- first method tried
- whether the method was efficient
- the idea that should have been seen earlier

Best rule:

- if you spend too long without progress, stop and classify the question before continuing

The skill you are building here is diagnosis, not just execution.

## Phase 3: Timed Paper Practice

Goal:

- combine method recognition, speed, and stamina

Suggested order:

1. practice and specimen papers
2. older papers
3. more recent papers
4. final mixed revision with the latest papers

Why this order:

- it reduces pressure at the start
- it gives you time to build confidence
- it saves the cleaner recent papers for more realistic final practice

## A Simple Weekly Plan

### Option A: Light Schedule

- Day 1: algebra drill
- Day 2: graph drill
- Day 3: trig or geometry drill
- Day 4: sequences or calculus drill
- Day 5: mixed set
- Day 6: review errors
- Day 7: rest or one short confidence set

### Option B: Stronger Schedule

- Day 1: algebra plus graphs
- Day 2: trig plus geometry
- Day 3: sequences plus calculus
- Day 4: number and logic plus review
- Day 5: mixed timed set
- Day 6: full paper
- Day 7: full review and targeted reattempts

## How To Review Properly

After each session, write down:

- the topic
- whether you got it right
- how long it took
- what the fastest valid method was
- what clue you missed

Useful labels for mistakes:

- `did not recognize topic`
- `used long method`
- `algebra slip`
- `bad diagram`
- `interval/counting mistake`
- `logic mistake`
- `careless reading`

## When To Move On

Move from topic drills to full papers when:

- you can usually identify the topic quickly
- you are no longer surprised by standard moves
- your errors come more from precision than from total confusion

If you still feel slow, do not just do more full papers.

Return to targeted drills for the weak topic.

## Final Aim

The goal is not only to know the maths.

The goal is to see quickly:

- what kind of question this is
- which method is shortest
- which traps are likely
- when to stop calculating and start reasoning
